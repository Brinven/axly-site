Why Every App I Build Has Zero Telemetry and One-Time Pricing

It's not an accident, it's the whole point.

If you've used anything from Axly's Customs you've probably noticed a pattern. No account required. No subscription. No "we may share your data with trusted partners." You pay once, you own it, and it runs on your machine.

That's not an oversight. It's a decision I made early and haven't changed.

I got tired of renting software

At some point, and I think a lot of people hit this wall, I looked at my monthly subscriptions and realized I didn't own anything. I was paying to access tools that could change their pricing, change their features, or just disappear. Tools that required an internet connection to work. Tools that were quietly sending data somewhere I couldn't see.

I build software for a living now. So I got to decide how mine would work.

Every app I ship runs locally. Your data stays on your device. There's no server I'm running that knows you exist. When you close the app, nothing phones home.

One-time pricing because I actually respect your budget

Subscriptions make sense for some businesses. If you're running infrastructure on my behalf, charge me monthly, fair enough.

But most apps don't do that. Most apps charge monthly because it's a better revenue model, not because it costs them anything per month to serve you. I don't think that's a good deal for users and I'm not interested in building my business on it.

Everything I sell is a one-time purchase. You pay, you own it, done. If I ship a major update that meaningfully changes what the app is, I might charge for that, but your original purchase doesn't expire and I don't hold features hostage behind a paywall.

The Texas law thing

Every app ships with a proper EULA and privacy policy, governed by Texas law. Not because lawyers told me to, I'm a solo developer, I'm not exactly drowning in legal counsel, but because being explicit about what the software does and doesn't do is just the right thing.

The privacy policy is short because there's not much to say. The app runs on your machine. It doesn't collect data. It doesn't have a backend watching you. That's it.

Local-first isn't a constraint, it's a feature

I've heard the argument that local-first software is harder to build and harder to monetize. Both are true. But there's something genuinely satisfying about shipping a tool that works on a plane, works when the API is down, works ten years from now when I've moved on to other things.

You own it. It runs. That's the deal.

It's the kind of software I wanted to use. So it's the kind of software I build.
